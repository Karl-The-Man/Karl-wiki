# Richard Feynman

American theoretical physicist (1918–1988). Nobel Prize in Physics (1965) for quantum electrodynamics. Professor at [[Caltech]]. Widely regarded as one of the greatest physicists and science communicators of the 20th century.

## Relevance to This Wiki

Feynman is the scientific exemplar of [[First Principles Thinking]]. Where [[Elon Musk]] and [[Jensen Huang]] apply first-principles reasoning to engineering and business, Feynman applies it to epistemology itself — asking "what is the essential ingredient that separates real knowledge from pseudoknowledge?"

His answer: [[Scientific Integrity]] — "a kind of utter honesty" that goes beyond not-lying to proactively disclosing how you might be wrong. This provides the *procedural standard* for truth-seeking that complements the *dispositional* accounts from Musk ([[Obsession with Truth]]), Huang ([[Gut-Check Resilience]]), and Graham ([[Independent-Mindedness]]).

## Key Contributions (per wiki sources)

### "The First Principle"

> "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."

This is Feynman's most famous formulation — a concise axiom for all reasoning. Self-deception, not external deception, is the primary enemy. This maps directly to Musk's [[Wishful Thinking]] warnings and Graham's concern with uncalibrated belief. *(Cargo Cult Science, 1974)*

### The Cargo Cult Metaphor

Feynman coins "[[Cargo Cult Science]]" for disciplines that imitate the *form* of scientific investigation without the *substance*. The metaphor — South Sea islanders building bamboo runways to attract planes — captures the gap between imitating a process and understanding why it works. This is [[Reasoning by Analogy]] applied to methodology: copying the external trappings of science rather than reasoning from its essential principles. *(Cargo Cult Science, 1974)*

### Scientific Integrity as Method

Unlike the other voices in this wiki, Feynman offers *procedural* standards for truth-seeking:
- Report everything that might make your results invalid
- Publish disconfirming evidence alongside confirming evidence
- Ensure your theory predicts things beyond the data that inspired it
- "Bend over backwards" to show how you might be wrong

See [[Scientific Integrity]].

### The Millikan Effect

Feynman describes how post-Millikan measurements of electron charge drifted gradually toward the correct value because experimenters anchored on Millikan's authority — discarding results that deviated too far. This is an empirical case study of [[Conventional-Mindedness]] in science and a warning about [[Reasoning by Analogy]] even within supposedly rigorous disciplines.
