# Conventional-Mindedness

The default human disposition: forming beliefs by absorbing the consensus of one's peers. [[Paul Graham]] treats it not as a flaw but as a spectrum position — adequate and even optimal for most work, but fatal for work that requires novelty.

## Definition

Conventional-minded people adopt the beliefs of their social group. "It genuinely feels to them as if they make up their own minds about everything. It's just a coincidence that their beliefs are identical to their peers'." This is not stupidity — it's a different cognitive strategy, one optimized for social coordination rather than discovery. *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

## Where It Suffices

Most work — administration, execution, coordination — requires only correctness, not novelty. "All you need is to be right. It's not essential that everyone else be wrong." For these domains, conventional-mindedness is no disadvantage and may even be an asset (you align with team norms more easily). *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

## Where It Fails

Work that requires novel insight — science, investing, startups, essays — penalizes conventional-mindedness. You can't profit from a prediction everyone shares; you can't publish findings everyone already knows; you can't build a startup around an idea everyone agrees is good ("or there will already be other companies doing it"). *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

## Relationship to Reasoning by Analogy

Conventional-mindedness is [[Reasoning by Analogy]] applied to beliefs rather than to engineering problems. Where Musk's "reasoning by analogy" copies *precedent* ("batteries have always been expensive"), conventional-mindedness copies *consensus* ("everyone knows X is true"). Both substitute social proof for independent verification. Both are cognitively cheap and usually adequate. Both become ceilings when novelty matters.

## The Self-Deception Problem

Conventional-minded people rarely identify as conventional-minded. Graham observes a possible Dunning-Kruger effect: "the most conventional-minded people are confident that they're independent-minded." Meanwhile, schools test intelligence but ignore independent-mindedness "except to the extent they try to suppress it," so people get no feedback on where they fall. *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

Strong convictions are not evidence of [[Independent-Mindedness]]: "Rather the opposite." Political extremists "think themselves nonconformists, but actually they're niche conformists. Their opinions may be different from the average person's, but they are often more influenced by their peers' opinions than the average person's are." *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

## Conformism in Organizations

As startups scale, they hire more conventional-minded people (who outnumber the independent-minded), diluting the founding culture. Possible mitigations Graham suggests:
- Hire only independent-minded people
- Create structural disruptions to conformism (like Lockheed's Skunk Works)
- Grow revenue without growing headcount
- Outsource roles that would bring conformism-prone employees into the culture
