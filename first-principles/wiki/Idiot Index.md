# Idiot Index

A manufacturing efficiency metric coined by [[Elon Musk]] at [[SpaceX]]: the ratio of a finished part's cost to the cost of its raw materials. A high ratio signals either over-complex design or inefficient manufacturing. "If the ratio is high, you're an idiot." *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## Formula

```
Idiot Index = Cost of finished part / Cost of raw materials
```

## How It's Used

Musk expects all engineers at [[SpaceX]] to know the best and worst parts in their systems as judged by the Idiot Index at all times. A high-scoring part triggers investigation: Is the design too complex? Is the manufacturing process inefficient? Can the part be redesigned or the process improved?

### Example
A rocket half-nozzle jacket cost $13,000 to produce but was made from only $200 worth of steel — an Idiot Index of 65. This flags the part for redesign or process overhaul. *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## Relationship to First Principles

The Idiot Index is a direct application of [[First Principles Thinking]] to manufacturing cost. By decomposing a part to its material constituents and pricing them on commodity exchanges, you establish the "magic wand number" — the theoretical cost floor if atom rearrangement were free. The gap between that floor and the actual cost is the space available for improvement.

For [[SpaceX]] rockets broadly, raw materials were only 1–2% of the finished cost, revealing that the industry's manufacturing practices were extraordinarily inefficient. *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## Generalizability

While developed for rocket manufacturing, the concept applies to any physical product. At [[Tesla]], the analogous first-principles analysis of battery materials ($80/kWh raw vs. $600/kWh finished) served the same diagnostic function. *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*
