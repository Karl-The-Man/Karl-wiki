# Gut-Check Resilience

A method for navigating crises by returning to core beliefs and checking whether the fundamentals that drove your decisions have actually changed. Practiced by [[Jensen Huang]] at [[NVIDIA]].

## The Method

When external conditions collapse (stock price, market sentiment, public perception), Huang's response is systematic:

1. **Return to core beliefs** — What do you believe? What are the most important things?
2. **Check fundamentals** — "Did physics change? Did gravity change? Did all of the things that we assumed that we believed that led to our decision, did any of those things change?"
3. **If fundamentals changed** → change everything
4. **If fundamentals haven't changed** → change nothing, keep going

> "The stock price changed, but did something else change? … Because if those things changed, you've got to change everything. But if none of those things changed, you change nothing, keep on going."
> *(Jensen Huang on How to Use First-Principles Thinking to Drive Decisions.md)*

## Case Study: NVIDIA's 80% Drop

During the financial crisis, NVIDIA lost 80% of its market cap. Wall Street didn't believe in their bet on machine learning. Huang's response:

- Woke up at the same time
- Prioritized his day the same way
- Kept the company focused on the core
- Maintained uniform emotional baseline ("my pulse was exactly the same")
- Showed up in front of employees despite the discomfort ("Leaders have to be seen, unfortunately")

He acknowledges the emotional reality — "it's a little embarrassing," "you don't want to get out of your bed" — but treats these as feelings to move through, not signals to act on.

## Connection to [[First Principles Thinking]]

Gut-check resilience is [[First Principles Thinking]] applied to crisis psychology. Rather than reacting to the surface event (stock price), you decompose to the axioms that generated your strategy and check if they still hold. The axioms — not the market's opinion — are the ground truth.

## Connection to [[Obsession with Truth]]

This method is truth-anchored: the question isn't "how do people feel about us?" but "are the things we believe still true?" It parallels [[Elon Musk]]'s insistence on checking axioms and guarding against [[Wishful Thinking]] — though in Huang's case, the wishful thinking to guard against is *pessimistic* (panicking because of market signals) rather than optimistic.
