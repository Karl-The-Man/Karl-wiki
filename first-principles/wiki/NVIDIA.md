# NVIDIA

GPU and accelerated computing company founded in 1993 by [[Jensen Huang]], Chris Malachowsky, and Curtis Priem. Funded by [[Sequoia Capital]] (Don Valentine). ~30,000 employees as of the interview date.

## Founding Mission

Build computers that solve problems normal computers can't. This mission has remained constant for 30+ years, applied successively to 3D graphics, computational drug design, weather simulation, robotics, autonomous driving, and artificial intelligence.

## First Principles in Practice

### Create Technology, Create Markets
NVIDIA's defining pattern: they don't just build products, they build the ecosystems those products require. This explains early and sustained investment in autonomous driving, deep learning (CUDA, cuDNN), and computational biology — all before meaningful markets existed.

### The OpenGL Pivot
In ~1995, NVIDIA's original 3D graphics technology became incompatible with Microsoft's Direct 3D standard. With 89 competitors and no knowledge of the "right" approach, Huang bought the OpenGL manual at Fry's Electronics and had his engineers implement the pipeline from scratch. This near-death experience became a foundational confidence builder: "You can succeed in doing something, inventing a future, even if you were not informed about it at all."

### [[EOIFS]] Culture
NVIDIA uses "Early Indicators of Future Success" rather than traditional KPIs to sustain long-term bets. For deep learning, the early indicator was [[Andrew Ng]]'s "fuzzy cat" — imperfect but directionally promising. This framework allowed NVIDIA to invest in accelerated computing for ~15 years before the AI market materialized.

### Importance Over Market Size
"We find inspiration, not from the size of a market, but from the importance of the work." No one at NVIDIA needs to present a P&L or financial forecast for a new project — only demonstrate the work is important and wouldn't happen without them.

## Organizational Design

Huang designed NVIDIA's flat structure from [[Organizational First Principles|first principles]]:
- 50 direct reports to the CEO
- Radical transparency — minimize "information mutilation" between layers
- Empowerment through shared context, not information hoarding
- "The smallest large company in the world"
