# Obsession with Truth

The epistemic disposition that [[Elon Musk]] describes as the prerequisite for [[First Principles Thinking]]: a pathological commitment to understanding what is actually true, regardless of what is comfortable or conventional.

> "I am obsessed with truth. Obsessed. If you're going to come up with a good solution, the truth is really, really important."
> *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## Why Physics

Musk studied physics specifically because it "attempts to understand the truth of the universe" and yields "provable truths with predictive power." He treats physics as the ultimate arbiter:

> "Physics is law. Everything else is a recommendation."

> "If you have beliefs that are incompatible with a rocket getting to orbit, the rocket will not get to orbit. Physics is a harsh judge."

## The Enemy: [[Wishful Thinking]]

Musk identifies [[Wishful Thinking]] as the primary cognitive threat. It manifests as:
- Filtering out inconvenient information
- Confusing perseverance with pursuing an unrealistic dream
- Believing things feel easy when they shouldn't
- Holding beliefs inversely proportional to evidence

He counteracts it with:
- Rigorous self-analysis
- Seeking feedback from all sources
- Assuming the team is "losing, even when it looks like we might win"
- Checking axioms: "Do you have the right fundamental axioms? Are they relevant? Are you making the right conclusions?"

## Aspiring to Be Less Wrong

Rather than claiming certainty, Musk frames the goal as continuous error reduction: "Aspire to be less wrong." He acknowledges that everyone makes mistakes — "It's just a question of how many and how often" — and that the real test is how quickly you adapt.

> "It's OK to be wrong. Just don't be confident and wrong."

This maps to a roughly Bayesian disposition: "Believe things proportionate to the evidence. Not inversely proportional to the evidence." *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## Huang's Version: "Did Physics Change?"

[[Jensen Huang]] expresses the same disposition differently. When [[NVIDIA]] lost 80% of its market cap, his crisis method was to return to fundamentals: "Did physics change? Did gravity change? Did all of the things that we assumed that we believed that led to our decision, did any of those things change?" If the axioms hold, the strategy holds — regardless of market sentiment. This is truth-obsession applied to resilience: feelings and stock prices are not evidence; only changes to the underlying truths matter. See [[Gut-Check Resilience]]. *(Jensen Huang on How to Use First-Principles Thinking to Drive Decisions.md)*
