## Central Thesis (Emerging)

[[First Principles Thinking]] is a reasoning method borrowed from physics: decompose a problem to its most fundamental, independently verifiable truths, then build upward. It contrasts with [[Reasoning by Analogy]] (copying precedent), which dominates daily life but constrains innovation. The method has been applied concretely to rocket manufacturing ([[SpaceX]]), battery costs ([[Tesla]]), and tunneling ([[The Boring Company]]), producing quantitative heuristics like the [[Idiot Index]] and the "magic wand number."

The method rests on an [[Obsession with Truth]] and a vigilance against [[Wishful Thinking]]. It is cognitively expensive and reserved for important, novel problems — routine decisions are better served by analogy.

## Key Themes

1. **Physics as epistemology** — The laws of physics set hard boundaries; everything else is negotiable. Treating physics as the foundation for reasoning produces better decisions in engineering and business.
2. **Decomposition reveals opportunity** — Breaking products into raw materials and pricing them on commodity exchanges exposes the gap between theoretical cost floors and current prices, quantifying the opportunity for innovation.
3. **Cognitive discipline** — First-principles thinking requires active resistance to wishful thinking, convention, and the cognitive ease of analogy.
4. **Self-directed learning** — Knowledge structured as a [[Semantic Tree of Knowledge|semantic tree]] (fundamentals first) enables autodidacts to reason from first principles in unfamiliar domains.
