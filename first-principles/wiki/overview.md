## Central Thesis (Emerging)

[[First Principles Thinking]] is a reasoning method borrowed from physics: decompose a problem to its most fundamental, independently verifiable truths, then build upward. It contrasts with [[Reasoning by Analogy]] (copying precedent), which dominates daily life but constrains innovation.

Three voices now anchor the wiki:

**[[Elon Musk]]** frames the method as axiomatic decomposition — identify your most confident truths, reason upward, check conclusions against axioms. Applied concretely to rocket manufacturing ([[SpaceX]]), battery costs ([[Tesla]]), and tunneling ([[The Boring Company]]), producing quantitative heuristics like the [[Idiot Index]].

**[[Jensen Huang]]** frames it as a reinvention prompt — learn how something was done, then reset given current conditions, tools, and motivations: "How would I reinvent this whole thing?" Applied to technology strategy, [[Organizational First Principles|organizational design]], [[EOIFS|metrics]], and reasoning about generative AI at [[NVIDIA]].

**[[Paul Graham]]** provides the *psychological layer* that Musk and Huang take for granted — explaining *who* can do first-principles thinking and *why*. He decomposes [[Independent-Mindedness]] into three interchangeable components: fastidiousness about truth (maps to [[Obsession with Truth]]), resistance to being told what to think, and curiosity (maps to [[Philosophy of Curiosity]]). He contrasts this with [[Conventional-Mindedness]] — adopting beliefs from social consensus — and identifies [[Intellectual Fashion]] as the propagation mechanism that makes convention the default.

Musk and Huang share an [[Obsession with Truth]] and a vigilance against [[Wishful Thinking]], but differ in emphasis: Musk stresses ignoring precedent from the start; Huang studies precedent first, then discards inherited constraints. Graham explains *why* most people can't do either: [[Conventional-Mindedness]] is the default, and without calibrated truth-seeking, even rebellion just produces conspiracy theorists. All three arrive at the same conclusion: convention is a ceiling, not a floor.

## Key Themes

1. **Physics as epistemology** — The laws of physics set hard boundaries; everything else is negotiable. Both Musk and Huang use physics as the foundation for reasoning. Huang's crisis test: "Did physics change? Did gravity change?"
2. **Decomposition reveals opportunity** — Breaking products into raw materials exposes the gap between theoretical cost floors and current prices (Musk). Learning existing approaches reveals which constraints are inherited vs. fundamental (Huang).
3. **Cognitive discipline** — First-principles thinking requires active resistance to wishful thinking, convention, and the cognitive ease of analogy.
4. **Independent-mindedness as prerequisite** — Graham's analysis reveals that first-principles thinking has a *psychological prerequisite*: [[Independent-Mindedness]]. Most people default to [[Conventional-Mindedness]] and cannot perform the method without cultivating truth-fastidiousness, resistance to conformism, and curiosity.
5. **Importance over market size** — Huang selects work based on importance, not addressable market: "The importance of the work is the early indicators of a future market." This complements Musk's focus on cost-floor analysis.
6. **Organizational application** — Huang extends first-principles thinking beyond engineering to management: design org charts from fundamentals, not from convention. 50 direct reports, radical transparency, empowerment through shared reasoning. Graham adds that organizational growth inevitably dilutes independent-mindedness.
7. **Resilience through axiom-checking** — Both practitioners use first-principles as a crisis tool: return to core beliefs, check if fundamentals changed, and if not, change nothing. See [[Gut-Check Resilience]].
8. **Intellectual fashion as obstacle** — Graham identifies [[Intellectual Fashion]] — waves of idea-conformism — as a primary barrier. Unfashionable ideas are "disproportionately likely to lead somewhere interesting."
