# EOIFS (Early Indicators of Future Success)

A decision-making framework used at [[NVIDIA]] to sustain conviction in long-term bets by decoupling evidence of doing the right thing from financial results. Coined and championed by [[Jensen Huang]].

## The Problem with KPIs

Huang finds traditional KPIs hard to interpret because they're typically *results* (e.g., gross margins), not leading indicators. By the time a result appears, you've either already succeeded or it's too late to course-correct. You need signals much earlier — signs that you're going in the right direction before the market validates you.

> "You want to decouple the result from evidence that you're doing the right things."
> *(Jensen Huang on How to Use First-Principles Thinking to Drive Decisions.md)*

## How It Works

1. **Start with a core belief** — a fundamental thesis about the future (e.g., "accelerated computing will solve problems CPUs can't").
2. **Look for early signals** — not revenue or margins, but evidence the thesis is directionally correct: research papers enabled by your technology, important problems solved, researchers adopting your tools.
3. **Use signals to sustain conviction** — unless something changes the core belief, continue. The signals give you (and your team) hope and direction without requiring market proof.

## Examples at NVIDIA

- **Deep learning**: The first EOIFS was [[Andrew Ng]]'s "fuzzy cat" — a neural network that could detect cats imperfectly but successfully enough to suggest the approach had potential. This predated any deep learning market by years.
- **cuDNN creation**: Researchers with "no money" needed a domain-specific language for neural network computing. NVIDIA built cuDNN not because it had a business case, but because the work was important. The adoption by researchers was the EOIFS.
- **Accelerated computing**: For 10–15 years, the only market was computer graphics. EOIFS (research papers, solved problems, researcher adoption) sustained NVIDIA's investment in a broader accelerated computing platform.

## The Importance Heuristic

Huang's selection criterion for what deserves investment:

> "We find inspiration, not from the size of a market, but from the importance of the work, because the importance of the work is the early indicators of a future market."

Paired with: "If we didn't do it, would it happen without us?" If the answer is yes, let someone else do it. If no, it's worth pursuing.

## Connection to [[First Principles Thinking]]

EOIFS is a first-principles approach to metrics: rather than accepting conventional measures of success (KPIs, P&Ls, financial forecasts), Huang reasons from fundamentals about what actually indicates you're on the right path. It's particularly valuable when pursuing [[First Principles Thinking]] in domains where the market doesn't yet exist to validate your reasoning.
