# Organizational First Principles

Applying [[First Principles Thinking]] to organizational design: rather than copying existing org charts, reason from what your company builds, the properties of its environment, and what behaviors you want to encourage.

## Huang's Argument

[[Jensen Huang]] observes that most companies organize the same way regardless of what they build — a hierarchical structure inherited from military organizations designed to minimize information flow to soldiers "whose fundamental purpose is to die in the field of battle, to die without asking questions."

> "If we all build different things, why are we organized the same way? Why would this organizational machinery be exactly the same irrespective of what you built?"
> *(Jensen Huang on How to Use First-Principles Thinking to Drive Decisions.md)*

## First-Principles Org Design Questions

Huang recommends asking:
- **What is the input? What is the output?** — What does this company actually produce?
- **What are the properties of this environment?** — Stable? Rapidly changing? Under attack?
- **What is the forest this animal has to live in?** — What characteristics determine survival?
- **What behaviors do we want to encourage vs. discourage?** — The "operating system" (culture).

## NVIDIA's Design

The answers led Huang to build [[NVIDIA]] as a radically flat organization:
- **50 direct reports** to the CEO — because people reporting to the CEO need the least management
- **Minimal layers** — reduces "information mutilation" between leadership and employees
- **Empowerment through shared context** — people can only make good decisions if they understand the situation; therefore, maximize transparency
- **No information-as-power** — Huang rejects the premise that a leader's value comes from possessing secret information

> "I would like us all to contribute to the company. And our position in the company should have something to do with our ability to reason through complicated things, lead other people to achieve greatness, inspire, empower other people, support other people."

## The Empowerment Loop

1. Share context (facts, data, assumptions, unknowns) transparently
2. Show how you reason through it (so people learn the method, not just the conclusion)
3. Trust people to make smart decisions on your behalf
4. Result: 30,000 people acting as empowered decision-makers

## Contrast with [[Reasoning by Analogy]]

Copying an org chart is classic analogical reasoning — "other companies do it this way, so we should too." Huang's approach decomposes to fundamentals: what is an organization *for*, and what design best serves that purpose for *this specific company*?
