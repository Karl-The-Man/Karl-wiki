# SpaceX

Aerospace company founded by [[Elon Musk]]. The decision to start SpaceX was itself driven by [[First Principles Thinking]]: a raw-material cost analysis showed rocket materials (aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber) comprised only 1–2% of finished rocket cost, revealing enormous room for manufacturing improvement. *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## First-Principles Origin Story

Most people assumed rockets would always be expensive because they had always been expensive ([[Reasoning by Analogy]]). Musk's analysis:
1. List all materials in a rocket and their quantities.
2. Price them at commodity rates.
3. Calculate the "magic wand number" — cost if atom rearrangement were free.
4. Result: well under 5% of current cost, sometimes 1–2%.

This gap — the [[Idiot Index]] applied to rocketry — proved radical cost reduction was physically possible, even if engineering it would be hard.

## Engineering Culture

- The [[Idiot Index]] became a general-purpose diagnostic for all parts. Engineers are expected to know the best and worst parts in their systems by this metric at all times.
- Example: a half-nozzle jacket cost $13,000 but contained only $200 of steel (Idiot Index: 65).
- When engineers say something is "impossible," Musk asks "What would it take?" to reframe the constraint.

## Learning Approach

Musk is self-taught in rocketry: "I just read a lot of books and talked to a lot of people." He started at the Palo Alto public library, reading about rocket engineering and borrowing old engine manuals from experts. *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*
