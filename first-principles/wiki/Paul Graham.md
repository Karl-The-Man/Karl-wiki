# Paul Graham

Essayist, programmer, and co-founder of Y Combinator. Graham writes influential essays on startups, technology, and thinking — his work sits at the intersection of epistemology and entrepreneurship.

## Relevance to This Wiki

Graham provides the **psychological and epistemic foundation** for [[First Principles Thinking]] that practitioners like [[Elon Musk]] and [[Jensen Huang]] take for granted. Where Musk describes *what* first-principles thinking is and Huang describes *how* to practice it, Graham explains *who* can do it and *why* — by dissecting the trait of [[Independent-Mindedness]] into three trainable components.

## Key Ideas

### Independent-Mindedness as a Spectrum

Graham argues that people fall on a spectrum from [[Conventional-Mindedness]] to [[Independent-Mindedness]], and that this placement determines what kind of work they should pursue. Work requiring novelty (science, investing, startups, essays) demands independent-mindedness; work requiring only correctness (administration, execution) does not. Picking the wrong type leads to chronic unhappiness. *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

### Three Components of Independent-Mindedness

1. **Fastidiousness about truth** — calibrated degrees of belief; horror of ideologies; essentially [[Obsession with Truth]]
2. **Resistance to being told what to think** — not mere contrarianism but an active delight in counterintuitive ideas; correlated with humor
3. **Curiosity** — the compass that finds new ideas to fill the space cleared by the other two; see [[Philosophy of Curiosity]]

These components are interchangeable — each can compensate for the others. *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

### Cultivation Techniques

- Surround yourself with independent-minded people
- Diversify your influences across groups and time periods (read history)
- Practice active skepticism: "Is that true?"
- Follow curiosity rather than passion: "Do what you're curious about"
- Avoid [[Intellectual Fashion]] — unfashionable ideas are disproportionately interesting

### On Startups and Conformism

As startups grow, the independent-minded founders get diluted by conventional-minded hires. Graham notes founders end up "able to speak more freely with founders of other companies than with their own employees." Possible solutions: hire only independent-minded people, outsource conformism-prone roles, grow revenue without growing headcount. *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*
