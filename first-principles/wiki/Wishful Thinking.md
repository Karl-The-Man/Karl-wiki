# Wishful Thinking

A cognitive bias that [[Elon Musk]] identifies as the primary enemy of sound decision-making: the tendency to believe things are the way you *wish* them to be, filtering out disconfirming information. *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## How It Manifests

- In business: underestimating difficulty, ignoring red flags, mistaking desire for evidence
- In personal life: confusing perseverance on a viable idea with chasing an unrealistic dream
- In engineering: assuming a design will work without checking against physics
- Universally: "You want things to be the way you wish them to be, so you tend to filter out information you shouldn't."

## Musk's Warning Signs

- Something "feels too easy"
- Something "doesn't quite make sense"
- Confident assertions without axiomatic grounding

## Countermeasures

Musk's toolkit for guarding against wishful thinking includes:
- [[Obsession with Truth]] — the dispositional antidote
- [[First Principles Thinking]] — forces grounding in verifiable truths
- Seeking negative feedback from all sources
- Assuming "we're losing, even when it looks like we might win"
- Rigorous self-analysis to distinguish belief from evidence

> "Do you have the right fundamental axioms, or truths? Are they relevant? Are you making the right conclusions based on those truths? That's the essence of critical thinking."
> *(elon-musk-on-first-principles.pdf)*

## Graham's Parallel: Uncalibrated Belief

[[Paul Graham]] describes the same phenomenon through the lens of [[Conventional-Mindedness]]: "For most people, degree of belief rushes unexamined toward the extremes: the unlikely becomes impossible, and the probable becomes certain." This uncalibrated belief is wishful thinking's mechanism — people slide toward comfortable certainty rather than maintaining the discomfort of probabilistic, evidence-proportionate belief. Graham's "fastidiousness about truth" (see [[Obsession with Truth]]) is the antidote: carefully labelling every belief with a considered degree of confidence. *(How to Think for Yourself.md)*

## Feynman's Treatment: Self-Deception as the First Principle

[[Richard Feynman]] elevates self-deception to the status of a first principle — the *primary* thing a thinker must guard against:

> "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."
> *(Cargo Cult Science, 1974)*

Feynman provides empirical evidence for how wishful thinking operates even within rigorous disciplines:

- **The Millikan effect:** After Millikan published a slightly-wrong measurement of electron charge, subsequent experimenters systematically discarded results that deviated too far from his value while accepting those that were close. "When they got a number that was too high above Millikan's, they thought something must be wrong — and they would look for and find a reason why something might be wrong. When they got a number closer to Millikan's value they didn't look so hard." This is wishful thinking operating through authority anchoring — *wanting* to agree with the established value.

- **Cargo cult dynamics:** Researchers in education, criminal justice, and parapsychology *want* their methods to work. This desire leads them to adopt the *forms* of scientific rigor without the substance — running experiments without proper controls, claiming results without replication. See [[Cargo Cult Science]].

Feynman's remedy — [[Scientific Integrity]] — is a procedural antidote: force yourself to report disconfirming evidence, publish both ways, and enumerate alternative explanations. Where Musk uses negative feedback ("assume we're losing"), Feynman uses disclosure ("bend over backwards to show how you're maybe wrong").
