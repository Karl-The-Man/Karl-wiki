# Scientific Integrity

A principle of thought defined by [[Richard Feynman]] as "a kind of utter honesty — a kind of leaning over backwards." It goes beyond mere honesty (not lying) to a proactive commitment to disclosing everything that might weaken your own position.

## Definition

> "The idea is to try to give all of the information to help others to judge the value of your contribution; not just the information that leads to judgment in one particular direction or another."
> *(Cargo Cult Science, 1974)*

Scientific integrity requires:

1. **Report disconfirming evidence.** If you make a theory, "you must also put down all the facts that disagree with it, as well as those that agree with it."
2. **Disclose alternative explanations.** Report "other causes that could possibly explain your results" and what you did to eliminate them.
3. **Ensure genuine prediction.** A theory must predict things beyond the data that inspired it — "the finished theory makes something else come out right, in addition."
4. **Publish both ways.** "If you've made up your mind to test a theory, you should always decide to publish it whichever way it comes out."
5. **Don't mislead laypersons.** Scientists should not exaggerate the applications of their work to secure funding.

## Contrast with Honesty

Feynman distinguishes scientific integrity from ordinary honesty using the Wesson Oil example: saying "Wesson oil doesn't soak through food" is technically true and not dishonest. But scientific integrity demands adding that *no* oil soaks through food at that temperature. "It's the implication which has been conveyed, not the fact, which is true, and the difference is what we have to deal with." *(Cargo Cult Science, 1974)*

## How It Connects to [[Obsession with Truth]]

[[Elon Musk]] and [[Jensen Huang]] describe the *disposition* of truth-seeking — the obsessive commitment to understanding what is actually true. Feynman provides the *procedure*: here is what you must actually *do* to avoid fooling yourself. The disposition without the procedure is incomplete — you can be obsessed with truth and still fool yourself through confirmation bias, authority-anchoring (the [[Millikan effect|Millikan effect]]), or institutional pressure.

[[Paul Graham]]'s "fastidiousness about truth" (see [[Independent-Mindedness]]) occupies the middle ground: more procedural than Musk/Huang ("calibrated degrees of belief," "Is that true?") but less systematic than Feynman's standards.

## Why It's Missing in [[Cargo Cult Science]]

Feynman's central argument is that pseudoscientific fields follow the *external forms* of science — experiments, statistics, peer review — but lack this internal integrity. The forms are visible and imitable; the integrity is invisible and cognitively expensive. This maps to [[Reasoning by Analogy]] applied to methodology: copying what science *looks like* rather than reasoning from what makes it *work*.

## Institutional Threats

Feynman identifies several ways institutions undermine scientific integrity:
- **Funding pressure:** The National Accelerator Laboratory wouldn't grant time for control experiments because they produced "no new results."
- **Authority anchoring:** The Millikan effect — experimenters unconsciously adjusted results toward the established value.
- **Novelty bias:** Mr. Young's foundational methodological work on rat experiments was ignored because it didn't discover anything about rats — only about how to run experiments correctly.

These institutional pressures map to Graham's [[Intellectual Fashion]] and [[Conventional-Mindedness]] — the social forces that make truth-seeking costly.
