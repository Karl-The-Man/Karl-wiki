# Cargo Cult Science (Richard Feynman)

**Source:** Caltech commencement address, June 14, 1974. Reprinted in *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!* (1985).

## Summary

[[Richard Feynman]] argues that the defining feature of real science — and the one most often missing from pseudoscience, social science, and institutional research — is **[[Scientific Integrity]]**: a "kind of utter honesty" that goes far beyond not lying. It means actively reporting everything that might make your results invalid, publishing disconfirming evidence alongside confirming evidence, and "bending over backwards" to show how you might be wrong. He coins the term "[[Cargo Cult Science]]" for disciplines that imitate the external forms of science (methodology, statistics, peer review) while lacking this essential integrity — like South Sea islanders building bamboo control towers and waiting for planes that never land.

## Key Claims

- **"The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."** Self-deception, not external deception, is the primary epistemic threat. This is Feynman's most famous line and his foundational axiom for all reasoning. See [[Obsession with Truth]], [[Wishful Thinking]].
- **Scientific integrity is not the same as honesty.** Honesty means not lying. Scientific integrity means proactively disclosing weaknesses, alternative explanations, and disconfirming data — "a kind of leaning over backwards." See [[Scientific Integrity]].
- **Most pseudoscience imitates form without substance.** Education research, criminal justice policy, parapsychology, and reflexology all adopt scientific trappings but lack the self-critical core. They are [[Cargo Cult Science]].
- **Institutional incentives actively destroy integrity.** The National Accelerator Laboratory prioritized "new results" for funding over allowing experimenters to run proper controls. Organizational pressure to produce results corrupts the truth-seeking process. See [[Conventional-Mindedness]], [[Intellectual Fashion]].
- **The Millikan effect demonstrates social anchoring of "truth."** After Millikan's slightly-wrong measurement of electron charge, subsequent experimenters drifted toward his number — dismissing results that deviated too far, accepting those that were close. Only gradually did the true value emerge. This is [[Reasoning by Analogy]] applied to data interpretation.
- **Mr. Young's rat experiment is the model of real science.** Young systematically eliminated every possible cue (paint, smell, light, sound) before claiming to have controlled for rat navigation — and was subsequently ignored because he didn't discover anything *about rats*, only about how to do experiments correctly. The field valued novelty over methodological rigor.
- **The responsibility extends beyond the lab.** Scientists should not mislead the public about the applications of their work, even to secure funding. "If you're representing yourself as a scientist, then you should explain to the layman what you're doing."

## Concepts Discussed

- [[Scientific Integrity]] — the core concept: utter honesty, proactive disclosure of disconfirming evidence
- [[Cargo Cult Science]] — imitating scientific form without scientific substance
- [[Obsession with Truth]] — Feynman's "first principle" is its most concise formulation
- [[Wishful Thinking]] — self-deception as the primary epistemic threat
- [[First Principles Thinking]] — the speech is a masterclass in decomposing "what makes science work" to its essential ingredient
- [[Reasoning by Analogy]] — the Millikan effect shows how authority anchors reasoning; cargo cults show how form-copying replaces substance
- [[Conventional-Mindedness]] — institutional pressure to conform to expected results (Millikan, NAL)
- [[Intellectual Fashion]] — novelty-seeking institutions ignore foundational methodological work (Mr. Young)
- [[Independent-Mindedness]] — Feynman's closing wish: "the good luck to be somewhere where you are free to maintain the kind of integrity I have described"

## Quotes Worth Preserving

> "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."

> "It's a kind of scientific integrity, a principle of scientific thought that corresponds to a kind of utter honesty — a kind of leaning over backwards."

> "Details that could throw doubt on your interpretation must be given, if you know them. You must do the best you can — if you know anything at all wrong, or possibly wrong — to explain it."

> "We've learned from experience that the truth will come out. Other experimenters will repeat your experiment and find out whether you were wrong or right."

> "So I have just one wish for you — the good luck to be somewhere where you are free to maintain the kind of integrity I have described, and where you do not feel forced by a need to maintain your position in the organization, or financial support, or so on, to lose your integrity."

## Strength/Weakness Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Feynman is arguably the 20th century's most celebrated first-principles thinker — his authority on this topic is unimpeachable.
- The speech provides *procedural* standards for truth-seeking (report disconfirming evidence, run controls, publish both ways) that Musk, Huang, and Graham lack — they describe the *disposition* but not the *method* of integrity.
- The cargo cult metaphor is extraordinarily vivid and has entered common usage precisely because it captures something real.
- The Millikan example is a devastating empirical demonstration of how even scientists fall prey to authority-anchored reasoning.
- The Mr. Young example shows what rigorous first-principles investigation actually looks like in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
- As a commencement speech, it's informal and anecdotal — no citations, no systematic argument.
- Feynman's examples are from the 1930s-1970s; the specific institutional dynamics have changed (though the patterns persist).
- The speech focuses almost entirely on what's *wrong* (pseudoscience, institutional corruption) rather than offering a positive program beyond "be honest."
- Feynman's dismissal of social sciences and psychology is broad — some of these fields have significantly improved their methodological rigor since 1974 (replication crisis reforms, pre-registration, etc.).
