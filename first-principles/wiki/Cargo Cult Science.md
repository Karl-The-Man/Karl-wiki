# Cargo Cult Science

A term coined by [[Richard Feynman]] for disciplines and practices that imitate the external forms of science — experiments, statistics, methodology, peer review — while lacking the essential ingredient that makes science work: [[Scientific Integrity]].

## The Metaphor

> During the war, South Sea islanders saw airplanes land with materials. After the war, they built bamboo runways, lit fires along the edges, carved wooden headphones, and waited. "The form is perfect. It looks exactly the way it looked before. But it doesn't work. No airplanes land."
> *(Cargo Cult Science, 1974)*

The islanders replicated every *visible* feature of an airstrip. What they couldn't replicate was the invisible infrastructure — radar, air traffic control, supply chains — that actually caused planes to arrive. Similarly, cargo cult scientists replicate the visible features of research while missing the invisible ingredient: the "kind of utter honesty" that constitutes [[Scientific Integrity]].

## Examples (per Feynman)

- **Education research:** "Big schools of reading methods and mathematics methods" that claim scientific backing, but reading scores "keep going down or hardly going up." The methods are never rigorously tested against alternatives.
- **Criminal justice policy:** "Lots of theory, but no progress in decreasing the amount of crime." Policies are justified with scientific language but not evaluated with scientific rigor.
- **Parapsychology (ESP research):** As methodological criticisms are addressed, effects shrink and disappear. Yet researchers reject the demand for repeatable experiments — "This is science?"
- **Reflexology:** Practitioners claim to diagnose the pituitary gland by rubbing a toe. No mechanism, no evidence, full confidence.

## Relationship to [[Reasoning by Analogy]]

Cargo cult science is [[Reasoning by Analogy]] applied to *methodology*: copying what science looks like rather than understanding what makes it work. Just as [[Elon Musk]] warns against copying competitor designs instead of reasoning from raw-material costs, Feynman warns against copying the *appearance* of rigor instead of reasoning from the *purpose* of rigor.

The key parallel: in both cases, the imitation is cognitively cheap and the real thing is cognitively expensive. [[First Principles Thinking]] (in engineering) and [[Scientific Integrity]] (in research) both require the discipline to go deeper than surface-level replication.

## Relationship to [[Conventional-Mindedness]]

[[Paul Graham]]'s description of [[Conventional-Mindedness]] maps cleanly onto cargo cult science: conventional-minded people "genuinely feel as if they make up their own minds about everything. It's just a coincidence that their beliefs are identical to their peers'." Cargo cult scientists genuinely feel they are doing science. It's just a coincidence that their methods lack the self-critical core.

## The Mr. Young Test

Feynman holds up Mr. Young's 1937 rat experiment as the anti-cargo-cult standard. Young systematically eliminated every possible cue (paint, smell, light, floor sound) before claiming control of rat navigation. The field ignored him — his work produced no novel discoveries about rats, only about how to do experiments correctly. "Not paying attention to experiments like that is a characteristic of cargo cult science." *(Cargo Cult Science, 1974)*

This is [[Intellectual Fashion]] in action: the field valued novelty over methodological rigor, exactly as Graham predicts.
