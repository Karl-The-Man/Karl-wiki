# From Hierarchy to Intelligence

**Author:** [[Jack Dorsey]] (@jack)
**Published:** 2026-03-31
**Source:** [block.xyz/inside/from-hierarchy-to-intelligence](https://block.xyz/inside/from-hierarchy-to-intelligence) (shared via X)

## Summary

[[Jack Dorsey]] argues that organizational hierarchy is fundamentally an [[Hierarchy as Information Routing|information routing protocol]] built around a fixed human limitation — a leader can manage 3–8 people — and that this constraint has governed every large organization for 2,000 years. [[Block]] is attempting to replace what hierarchy does with AI, building a [[Company as Intelligence|company organized as an intelligence]] rather than a hierarchy. The essay traces the history of organizational design from the Roman Army through the Prussian General Staff, American railroads, Frederick Taylor, the matrix organization, and modern tech experiments (Spotify, Zappos, Valve), showing that none broke the underlying span-of-control tradeoff. Block's model replaces hierarchy with two [[World Model (Organizational)|world models]] (company + customer), an [[Intelligence Layer]] that composes atomic capabilities into proactive solutions, and a flat structure of three roles: ICs, DRIs, and player-coaches.

## Key Claims

- **Speed is the best predictor of startup success** (Sequoia observation), and hierarchy is the primary impediment to speed because it slows information flow
- **Organizational hierarchy is an information routing protocol**, not a management philosophy — it exists because humans can only manage 3–8 direct reports, forcing layers that slow information
- **2,000 years of organizational innovation** (Roman contubernium → Prussian General Staff → railroad org charts → Taylor's scientific management → matrix orgs → Spotify squads → Holacracy) have all failed to break this fundamental tradeoff
- **Block is building a "company as intelligence"** — replacing human coordination layers with AI that maintains a continuously updated model of the entire business
- **Two world models** power the system: a *company world model* (operations, performance, priorities — replacing what managers carry in their heads) and a *customer world model* (per-customer, per-merchant financial reality built from transaction data)
- **Remote-first is a structural prerequisite**: everything creates machine-readable artifacts, providing raw material for the company world model
- **Four-layer architecture**: (1) Capabilities — atomic financial primitives with no UIs, (2) World Model — company + customer, (3) Intelligence Layer — composes capabilities into proactive solutions, (4) Interfaces — delivery surfaces (Square, Cash App, etc.)
- **The intelligence layer replaces the product roadmap**: when it tries to compose a solution and can't because a capability doesn't exist, that failure signal *is* the roadmap
- **Three roles replace the hierarchy**: ICs (deep specialists building capabilities/model/intelligence/interfaces), DRIs (own cross-cutting problems for ~90 days with authority to pull resources), player-coaches (combine building with developing people)
- **No permanent middle management layer** — the world model handles alignment, DRIs handle priority, player-coaches handle craft and people
- **If AI is just cost optimization, you get absorbed**: "You cut headcount, improve margins for a few quarters, and eventually get absorbed by something smarter"
- **Money is the most honest signal in the world**: transactions are facts about someone's life — Block sees both sides (buyer via Cash App, seller via Square)

## Strength/Weakness Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Compelling historical sweep — tracing hierarchy from Roman military through railroads to modern tech gives the argument genuine depth and makes the case that this is a *2,000-year constraint* being broken, not just a management fad
- The "information routing protocol" reframe is genuinely clarifying — it explains *why* hierarchy exists (not power, not tradition, but bandwidth limitations) and therefore *what* AI needs to replace
- Concrete architectural vision: four layers (capabilities, world model, intelligence, interfaces) and three roles (ICs, DRIs, player-coaches) — this isn't just theory
- The "failure signal as roadmap" concept is powerful — the intelligence layer's inability to compose a solution generates the backlog directly from customer reality
- Honest about difficulty: "parts of it will likely break before they work"

**Weaknesses:**
- Block is "in the early stages" — this is a vision document, not a case study. No concrete metrics on how the transition is going, what has worked, what has broken
- The transaction data moat argument is specific to fintech — the "customer world model" built from payment data doesn't generalize easily to companies without that signal density
- Doesn't address the obvious counterargument: hierarchy also provides accountability, career progression, mentorship, and political protection — functions that go beyond information routing
- The Spotify/Zappos/Valve dismissal is brief — "they failed because they lacked AI" is convenient but underexplored. Those experiments may have failed for reasons AI doesn't solve (politics, accountability, decision rights)
- No discussion of what happens when the world model is wrong, or when the intelligence layer composes a harmful solution — the safety/governance layer is absent
