# The Printing Press Analogy

[[Boris Cherny]]'s central metaphor for understanding the current AI moment: software engineers are the modern equivalent of medieval scribes, and AI is the printing press.

## The Analogy

**Before the printing press (1400s):**
- A tiny elite of scribes knew how to write
- Often the king themselves was illiterate — they employed scribes because they couldn't write
- Scribes held a rare, valuable, gatekeeping skill

**After the printing press:**
- Cost of printed material dropped **~100x** in 30–50 years
- Quantity increased **~10,000x** in 50–100 years
- Global literacy eventually reached ~70%, but took **200–300 years**
- Scribes didn't disappear — they became **writers and authors**
- The market for literature expanded enormously
- Effects were unpredictable and took centuries to fully manifest

**The parallel today:**
- Software engineers are the scribes — a small group with a rare skill (coding)
- Business owners who know what they want to build employ engineers because they cannot code themselves
- AI is the printing press — democratizing the ability to create software
- Engineers won't disappear; they'll evolve into new roles as the market for software expands massively

## Significance

The analogy serves multiple purposes:

1. **Calms fears of displacement**: scribes became authors in a much larger market
2. **Sets expectations for timeline**: the printing press took centuries to fully transform society; AI's effects will also unfold over time
3. **Emphasizes unpredictability**: just as the printing press's full effects were unimaginable to 15th-century scribes, AI's applications will surprise us
4. **Reframes identity**: the skill shifts from the mechanics (writing/coding) to the creative and strategic layer above it
