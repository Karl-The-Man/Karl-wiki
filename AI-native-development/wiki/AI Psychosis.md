# AI Psychosis

[[Andrej Karpathy]]'s term for the perpetual, overwhelming awareness of what's now possible with AI agents — and the anxiety that you're not pushing hard enough to exploit it.

## The Experience

> "I kind of feel like I was just in this perpetual — I still am often in this state of AI psychosis all the time. Because there was a huge unlock in what you can achieve as a person, as an individual."

The psychosis has multiple components:

1. **Possibility overload** — everything is unexplored; there are always more agents to spin up, more things to automate
2. **[[Skill Issue]] framing** — when something doesn't work, it feels like your failure to prompt/orchestrate correctly, not a capability limitation
3. **Subscription anxiety** — feeling nervous when you have unused token capacity, similar to PhD students feeling anxious when GPUs are idle
4. **FOMO** — "I see lots of people on Twitter doing all kinds of things, and they all sound like really good ideas, and I need to be at the forefront or I feel extremely nervous"
5. **Addiction** — "it's very addictive, because there's unlocks when you get better"

## The Loopy Era

Karpathy describes layers of abstraction that keep stacking, each one taken for granted before the next appears:

> "The LLM part is now taken for granted. The agent part is now taken for granted. Now the claw-like entities are taken for granted. And now you can have multiple of them, and now you can have instructions to them, and now you can have optimization over the instructions... this is infinite and everything is skill issue."

This infinite recursion — the feeling that there's always another layer to optimize — is the core of the psychosis.

## Not Unique to Karpathy

The No Priors hosts describe a team at Conviction where "none of the engineers write code by hand, and they're all microphoned, and they just whisper to their agents all the time." Even Jensen Huang acknowledged the phenomenon: "tools were all just busier, unfortunately."
