# Hierarchy as Information Routing

The insight that organizational hierarchy is fundamentally an **information routing protocol** built around a fixed human limitation: a leader can effectively manage 3–8 people (span of control). This reframe is the analytical foundation of [[Jack Dorsey]]'s [[From Hierarchy to Intelligence]].

## The Core Constraint

Hierarchy doesn't exist because it's the best organizational form. It exists because of a bandwidth limitation: humans can only hold context on a small number of direct reports. To coordinate thousands of people, you need layers. More layers means slower information flow. This is the tradeoff that has governed every large organization for 2,000 years.

> "Companies move fast or slow based on information flow. Hierarchy and middle management impede information flow."

## 2,000 Years of Attempts

### Roman Army (~200 BC)
The first documented hierarchical organization at scale. Structure: 8 (contubernium) → 80 (century) → 480 (cohort) → 5,000 (legion). Each layer: a commander with defined authority, aggregating information from below, relaying decisions from above. "An information routing protocol built around a simple human limitation."

### Prussian General Staff (1806+)
After Napoleon destroyed the Prussian army at Jena, reformers (Scharnhorst, Gneisenau) created the General Staff — trained officers whose job was not to fight but to plan, process information, and coordinate. This was **middle management before the term existed**: "Professionals whose purpose was to route information, pre-compute decisions, and maintain alignment." Also formalized "line" (core mission) vs. "staff" (specialized support) — vocabulary still used today.

### American Railroads (1840s–1850s)
West Point engineers brought military organizational thinking to railroads. Daniel McCallum created the **world's first organizational chart** (1854) for the New York and Erie Railroad — formalizing the same layered hierarchy for a civilian enterprise. Train collisions were killing people; informal management failed at scale.

### Frederick Taylor (1856–1915)
"Scientific management" optimized work *within* the hierarchy: break work into specialized tasks, assign to trained experts, manage through measurement. Produced the functional pyramid. Didn't change the routing protocol — refined what happened inside it.

### Manhattan Project (1940s)
First stress test of functional hierarchy under extreme cross-disciplinary pressure. Oppenheimer organized Los Alamos into functional divisions but forced open collaboration across them. When the implosion problem became critical (1944), he reorganized around it — cross-functional teams unlike anything in corporate America. It worked, but as a "wartime exception led by a singular figure."

### Matrix Organization (1959+)
McKinsey's Gilbert Clee and Alfred di Scipio provided the framework; Marvin Bower helped companies (Shell, GE) implement it. Combined functional specialties with divisional units. The McKinsey 7-S framework (Peters, Waterman, late 1970s) added "soft Ss" (culture, skills, style) to "hard Ss" (strategy, structure, systems). Became the "modern corporation" — but didn't break the span-of-control constraint.

### Modern Tech Experiments
- **Spotify**: cross-functional squads with sprint cycles → moved back toward conventional management at scale
- **Zappos**: Holacracy, eliminating management titles → significant attrition
- **Valve**: flat structure, no formal hierarchy → difficult to scale beyond a few hundred people

> "As organizations grow into the thousands, they revert to hierarchical coordination because no alternative information routing mechanism has been powerful enough to replace it."

## Why AI Changes This

Previous attempts at post-hierarchy organization failed because they lacked a technology capable of performing the coordination function that hierarchy provides. AI is that technology because it can:

- Maintain a continuously updated model of an entire business ([[World Model (Organizational)|company world model]])
- Provide every person the context they need without waiting for information to travel through a chain of command
- Coordinate work that previously required humans relaying information through layers of management

This doesn't eliminate all management functions (mentorship, ethical judgment, novel situations). It eliminates the **information routing** function that created the need for layers.
