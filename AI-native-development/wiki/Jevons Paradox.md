# Jevons Paradox

When a resource becomes cheaper to use, total consumption of that resource increases rather than decreases — because reduced cost unlocks previously suppressed demand. Applied by [[Andrej Karpathy]] to the future of software engineering.

## The Software Argument

> "The reason we don't have more demand for software is just scarcity and it's too expensive. So if the barrier comes down, then actually you have the Jevons paradox — the demand for software goes up."

Karpathy is "cautiously optimistic" about software engineering jobs because:
- Software becomes dramatically cheaper to produce
- But demand for software is enormous and currently suppressed by cost
- Cheaper production → more software gets built → more demand for people who can direct it

## The ATM Precedent

The classical example: ATMs were expected to displace bank tellers. Instead, ATMs made bank branches cheaper to operate → more branches opened → more tellers were hired. The technology didn't reduce employment — it expanded the market.

## Code as Ephemeral

> "Code is now ephemeral, and it can change, and it can be modified. You're not forced to subscribe to what exists."

When software is cheap enough to be disposable and custom, the demand curve explodes — everyone gets bespoke solutions instead of adapting to generic tools.

## Caveats

Karpathy qualifies this as a near-term view:
- "Right now at least locally, there's going to be more demand for software"
- Long-term: frontier lab researchers "are automating themselves away actively"
- "The long-term future of that is uncertain... it's a job of economists to do properly"

## Connection to The Printing Press Analogy

[[The Printing Press Analogy]] (from [[Boris Cherny]]) makes a compatible argument: the printing press didn't eliminate scribes — it created a vastly larger market for written content, and scribes evolved into authors. Jevons Paradox is the economic mechanism underlying that historical parallel.
